Abstract

Audio-visual segmentation (AVS) seeks to localize sounding objects within visual frames by leveraging acoustic cues. Existing models are typically optimized via empirical risk minimization, which exploits dataset-level co-occurrence biases and temporal heuristics rather than intrinsic physical mechanisms, leading to generalization degradation under distribution shifts. To address this, we formulate the AVS task via structural causal models, identifying unobserved spatial global context and temporal salient motion as distinct confounders that establish spurious shortcuts. We propose the decoupled causal flow (DCF) framework to approximate interventional distributions without any external foundation models. At the holistic level, we apply the calculus of intervention via dynamic memory banks to sample from marginalized contextual distributions, constructing multi-modal counterfactual sets. At the representation level, we establish that standard spatial attention amplifies confounding biases and thereby introduce a differentiable bi-directional subspace factorization to isolate the low-rank causal subspace from high-rank contextual noise in the spectral domain. Beyond passive observation, we actively synthesize spatiotemporal counterfactuals by structurally disrupting endogenous fine-grained physical topologies and severing temporal Copula dependencies. These multi-level interventions are unified through a hierarchical causal alignment objective. By bounding the spurious total indirect effect driven by synergistic environmental confounding, DCF enforces cross-modal representation invariance and predictive confidence consistency. Extensive experiments on AVS benchmarks demonstrate that our framework effectively suppresses spatiotemporal confounders and achieves state-of-the-art performance.
